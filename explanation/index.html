EXPLANATION

Nary's Income After Kurt Dies


After Kurt dies, Nary's income will come from:

  • Social Security payments based on lifetime work earnings of Kurt
  • Social Security payments based on lifetime work earnings of Nary
  • Withdrawal of funds from Nary's Individual Retirement Account (IRA) inherited from Kurt

There are 2 kinds of Social Security benefits:

  • Spousal Benefits - while Kurt is alive
  • Survivor Benefits - subsequent to Kurt's death

Social Security Spousal Benefit - While Kurt is Alive


Kurt's monthly gross Social Security benefit is $4,029.
If Kurt is alive when Nary reaches age 67 then Nary will receive her Spousal Benefit. This benefit is 50% of Kurt's monthly benefit or $2,015. Nary may take this Spousal Benefit as early as age 62, but the amount will be reduced forever.

So Nary's Social Security Spousal Benefit will be $24,180 per year until Kurt dies.

Social Security Survivor Benefit - After Kurt Has Died


Kurt's monthly gross Social Security benefit is $4,029.
If Kurt dies after Nary reaches age 67 then Nary will receive her Survivor Benefit. This benefit is 100% of Kurt's monthly benefit or $4,029. Nary may take this Survivor Benefit as early as age 60, but the amount will be reduced forever.

So Nary's Social Security Survivor Benefit will be $48,348 per year subsequent to Kurt's death.

Individual Retirement Account (IRA)

Because I have not removed money from the IRA, it has accumulated by investment and compounded interest over many years. The IRA belongs 80% to my wife and %20 to my daughter.

Consequence of Taking Money Out of the IRA


Suppose the goal is to get $100,000 from the IRA to use for some purpose.
But it is necessary to take more out of the IRA to pay the tax on the withdrawal. The current tax bracket for us is 24% in 2022.

Let X be the amount that has to be withdrawn in order to net $100,000.
Then X - (.24X) = 100,000
X = 131,579

So to pay taxes on the $100,000 it is necessary to take $131,579 out of the IRA.

Because the IRA has $131,579 less money, over 10 years the IRA will have lost a total of $45,252 in compound interest assuming an interest rate of 3% each year. So the total money lost to the IRA is $131,579 + $45,252 = $176,831 in order to get $100,000
